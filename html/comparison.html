Comparison

Comparisons are made among available treatment options on several attributes including Cost of treatment, Risk of side effects, Treatment precautions, Thyroid hormone control and Risk of serious medical conditions.
Type of treatments Cost of treatment
Anti-thyroid drugs (propylthiouracil and carbimazole)
  • Cheapest option
Radioactive Iodine (RAI)
  • -N/A-
Thyroidectomy
  • Expensive cost
Type of treatments Risk of side effects
Anti-thyroid drugs (propylthiouracil and carbimazole)
  • Risk of minor allergic side effects such as rashes. 5 out of 100 patients get this; 95 out of 100 do not.
Radioactive Iodine (RAI)
  • Loss of taste and dry mouth due to salivary gland damage may happen.
Thyroidectomy
  • Risk of transient (temporary) vocal cord paralysis in 3 out of 100 patients who undergo surgery; 97 out of 100 do not get this.
Type of treatments Treatment precautions
Anti-thyroid drugs (propylthiouracil and carbimazole)
  • -N/A-
Radioactive Iodine (RAI)
  • You will have to comply with strict radiation precaution rules for about 1-5 days (e.g. keeping a distance from children and pregnant women, sleeping in separate beds)
Thyroidectomy
  • You would have to rest after the surgery and may be on medical leave for 1-2 weeks.
Type of treatments Thyroid hormone control
Anti-thyroid drugs (propylthiouracil and carbimazole)
  • Avoid long term hypothyroidism (permanently low levels of thyroid hormones).

  • Risk of hyperthyroidism relapse. About 1 in 2 people on oral medication will get a relapse of hyperthyroidism.
Radioactive Iodine (RAI)
  • Permanent remission (stop) of hyperthyroidism. With adjustment and monitoring of your oral thyroxine doses, you can achieve normal thyroid hormone levels.

  • You will have permanent hypothyroidism requiring lifelong oral thyroxine hormone and annual T4 blood test monitoring (the test for thyroid hormone levels)
Thyroidectomy
  • Permanent remission (stop) of hyperthyroidism. With adjustment and monitoring of your oral thyroxine doses, you can achieve normal thyroid hormone levels.

  • You will have permanent hypothyroidism requiring lifelong oral thyroxine hormone and annual T4 blood test monitoring (the test for thyroid hormone levels)

  • Recurrent (relapse) hyperthyroidism in 2 out of 100 patients who undergo surgery; 98 out of 100 do not get this.
Type of treatments Risk of serious medical conditions
Anti-thyroid drugs (propylthiouracil and carbimazole)
  • Risk of agranulocytosis (a serious condition with a severe decrease in the production of white blood cells). 37 out of 10000 patients receiving propylthiouracil get this (9963 out of 10000 do not get it) and in 35 out of 10000 receiving methimazole get this (9965 out of 10000 do not)

  • Risk of serious life-threatening allergic skin condition called Steven-johnson syndrome. 45 out of 10000 patients get this; 9955 out of 10000 do not.
Radioactive Iodine (RAI)
  • Risk of Graves’ ophthalmopathy (a condition affecting the area around your eye, where the eyelids retract and the eyes appear to bulge out). This condition happens in 15 out of 100 patients who use RAI (and does not happen in 85 out of 100). For patients with existing Graves’ ophthalmopathy, the condition worsens in 36 out of 100; 64 out of 100 do not get worse.

  • There is a slight increase risk of cancer after exposure to radioactive iodine. 154 out of 10 000 people treated with RAI develop cancer compared to 126 out of 10 000 who are not treated with RAI.
Thyroidectomy
  • Prolonged postoperative hypocalcemia (low calcium levels in 3 out of 100 patients who undergo surgery; 97 out of 100 do not get this.

  • Permanent hypoparathyroidism (low levels of the parathyroid hormone, which regulates calcium absorption) in 1 out of 100 patients who undergo surgery; 99 out of 100 do not get this.
